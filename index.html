Apprenticeships and OTJ

An Apprenticeship?

An apprenticeship is a work-based training programme where individuals learn a skilled trade or profession whilst earning a wage. It combines practical on-the-job experience with classroom instruction, leading to a recognised qualification. It offers structured learning, mentorship, and career advancement opportunities in various industries. Apprenticeships benefit both individuals seeking valuable skills and employers looking to build a skilled workforce.

What do they involve?

Apprenticeships typically last between one and four years, depending on the skill level and industry. During this time, apprentices work alongside experienced professionals, gaining hands-on skills and knowledge relevant to their chosen field. This blended learning approach ensures apprentices acquire both the practical abilities and the academic knowledge needed to succeed in their careers. Many employers also offer apprentices permanent positions after they successfully finish their training.

Off-The-Job Hours

A key requirement of UK apprenticeships is off-the-job training (OTJT), mandating a minimum of 20% of a full-time apprentice's working hours, averaging 6 hours per week. This minimum is crucial as it ensures dedicated time for apprentices to acquire the new knowledge, skills, and behaviours essential for their apprenticeship standard, beyond their routine work tasks, ultimately contributing to their competence and the value of the qualification.

Push it to the minimum!

In the UK, apprenticeships legally require a minimum of 20% of a full-time apprentice's working hours to be dedicated to off-the-job training (OTJT), averaging at least 6 hours per week, to ensure structured learning of new knowledge, skills, and behaviours relevant to their apprenticeship standard, distinct from their regular work duties. This protected learning time is crucial for developing competence, meeting assessment criteria, and ultimately contributing to the apprentice's qualification and long-term career prospects, with both employers and providers responsible for its delivery and recording.

The minimum off-the-job training (OTJT) requirement in UK apprenticeships is vital for several key reasons: it guarantees dedicated time for apprentices to acquire new knowledge and skills beyond their immediate job tasks, ensuring a well-rounded learning experience that develops genuine competence and a deeper understanding of their field.
